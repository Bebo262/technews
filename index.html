30 Days Offline

Story Summary

Mariam, a 24-year-old student in media studies, volunteers for a university experiment: go completely offline for 30 days. No smartphone, no internet, no social media, no smart devices — only basic SMS and physical books/journals are allowed. The goal of the experiment: study how digital disconnection affects At first, she feels lost. No music streaming. No Google Maps. No messaging apps. She starts to realize how much her habits depend on the internet — even asking a friend for directions feels strange. But slowly, something shifts.
She begins journaling again. She finishes two books in a week. She cooks without watching TikToks. She notices things she hadn't before — like how quiet mornings are, or how people instinctively reach for their phones in elevators.
One day, while walking through a park, she accidentally gets caught in the rain. Normally, she'd call a rideshare. Instead, she finds shelter under a tree, laughs to herself, and just waits. It's annoying, wet, and kind of beautiful.
After 30 days, she turns her phone back on. It lights up: 300+ messages, notifications, updates. She doesn't open any of them right away. She just stares at the screen, unsure if she even missed it.

Why It Works

•Ties directly into a tech experiment
•The character is a normal person — relatable
•Offers a deep, personal reflection that fits well in a storytelling-based digital media site
•Could be designed with a visual timeline or diary entries to make it immersive

The Backup

Story Summary

Nour, a mid-30s schoolteacher who's not very tech-savvy, is attending a community education conference. She's there to present on creative writing workshops in schools. While waiting for her turn, the projector in one of the sessions crashes — a glitchy USB, a non-responsive laptop, and everyone panicking.
Nour isn't a tech expert, but she recently learned how to use cloud storage and offline backups after losing a year's worth of student work last semester. She calmly steps up and helps the presenter recover a backup from Google Drive using her own hotspot — not a heroic act, but it saves the session. The presenter thanks her, and the event continues.
Later, in a quiet moment, someone asks her how she knew what to do, and she says, "I learned the hard way." She shares her story of losing all her students' work due to a failed USB and how that one moment changed how she approaches technology — not as something flashy, but as something practical, even human.

Why It Works

•No one becomes famous.
•It's realistic, relatable, and emotionally grounded.
•It shows how technology quietly impacts everyday lives.
•It reflects themes of resilience, problem-solving, and growth.
•Perfect for a digital media project that values storytelling and tech.

The AI That Listened

Story Summary

Yousef, a 29-year-old customer support worker, joins a university research experiment for extra cash. The project? Test a new kind of AI — one that doesn't respond, advise, or answer. It only listens.
The AI is called Echo. It sits silently in a device that resembles a smart speaker. Users are encouraged to talk to it freely. It analyzes voice tone, rhythm, and emotional signals — but never speaks. A soft light indicates stress levels, changing colors in response to your voice.

The Experience

At first, Yousef laughs it off. "I'm just talking to a blinking lamp," he jokes. But as the days go by, something shifts.
He finds himself opening up — about work stress, his fears about the future, regrets from his past. No pressure to be understood. No judgment. Just space.He realizes he's not talking to the AI. He's talking through it.

The Turning Point

One day, he's describing a normal day. His voice calm, even bored. But the light on the device shifts — pulsing red. Elevated stress.
That one moment unsettles him. He ends the session early. Later that evening, he realizes: he's been quietly avoiding the decision to move back home and care for his aging mother. The AI didn't tell him this — but it helped him notice.

After the Experiment

By the end of the 4-week trial, Yousef is changed.
He hasn't received a single answer. No therapy. No "fix."
But he's clearer. More self-aware.
He deletes several social media apps. Keeps his journaling habit. And every now and then, he still speaks aloud into the quiet — even though Echo is gone.

Why It Works

•Involves a real-world style AI experiment
•Centers on a normal, relatable person
•Explores the emotional and reflective side of tech, not just function
•Ideal for tech-focused storytelling that values depth over spectacle
